EDA

This is the EDA section of our website.
What is EDA? This stands for Exploratory Data Analysis. It is the process of sorting through data and determining patterns. This is very important because it allowed us to explore this happiness data and continue to discover new ways to display its trends.
The bar chart shows the happiness score of each country from highest to lowest. Not all of the countries are listed below, so hover over a bar to see the country and its exact happiness score. In 2022, Finland had the highest happiness score (7.8), and Afghanistan had the lowest (2.4)

The histogram below displays the range in happiness score, or ladder score, for the countries . If you hover over a bar, it will tell you the number of countries that had a score between two values. The most common range was between 6 and 6.5, with 30 countries in that range.

The heat map below shows the correlation between different factors. Lighter colors show a higher correlation (more of an effect on happiness scores), and darker colors mean lower correlation (less of an effect). For example, GDP and social support have a high effect on happiness scores, meanwhile, Generosity doesn't seem to matter at all.

The scatter plot below shows the happiness score and life expectancy of countries. It seems that generally higher life expectancy leads to a higher happiness score.

This visualization was made so we could show how happiness scores varied across the world using a map. As you can see from the key on the side, higher happiness scores are more yellow, and lower happiness scores are more purple. Also, if you hover over a country, the map will show the country's name and its exact happiness score. From this map, it seems that North America and Europe are the happiest continents.

Model Choices

We have chosen various regression models such as the Linear Regression, SVM, Decision Tree Regressor, Random Forest Regressor, Elastic Net, K Nearest Neighbors Regressor, Neural Networks (MLP Regressor), and XG Boost (Gradient Boosting) model. It is important for us to create numerous models so we can obtain the model with the most accurate prediction or the best accuracy. The score that we want to attain shows us how closely related our predictions and the y_test are. All of the models we have used are supervised machine learning models. Down below are details, that were found on https://scikit-learn.org/, on each of the models we have used.

Linear Regression: Linear Regression models show linear relationship between two variables.

SVM: SVM models or Support Vector Machine models are models that can be used for regression, classification, and outlier detection.

Decision Tree Regressor: The Decision Tree Regression model is used for both regression and classification. This model splits your data into smaller subcategories to make a model presented in a tree-like structure. This model is also very simple to interpret and understand.

Random Forest Regressor: Each tree in the ensemble in a Random Forest Regression model is created from a sample drawn with replacement from the training set. Random Forest models are a collection of decision trees that only focus on one output.

Elastic Net: Elastic Net is a type of Linear Regression model that is used when there are multiple attributes correlated to each other. It is trained with both L1 and L2 -norm regularization of the coefficients.

K Nearest Neighbor Regressor: K Nearest Neighbor Regression model is a simple model that classifies certain points to a group based on the mean of the label of its neighbors.

Neural Networks (MLP Regressor): A Neural Network model is basically a simplified model of how a human brain processes information. The model simulates many processing units that are arranged in layers.

XG Boost (Gradient Boosting): The Gradient Boosting model is a histogram-based estimator that is very fast with large samples.
